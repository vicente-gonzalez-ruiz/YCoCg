1 The \(\text {RGB} \Leftrightarrow \text {YCoCg}\) transform

Clearly, orthogonality is a desired property in lossy compression systems because it helps to isolate1 the impact (in the case of a color transform) of each color subband on the quality of the reconstruction of the image, simplifying significantly the determination of the pattern of QSs that generate the optimal RD curve.

Moreover, Eqs. ?? and ?? were derived by Principal Component Analysis (PCA) on old2 video data. The same procedure has been carried out with newer3 images, obtaining \begin {equation} \begin {bmatrix} \text {Y} \\ \text {C}_1 \\ \text {C}_2 \end {bmatrix} = \begin {bmatrix} \frac {1}{3} & \frac {1}{3} & \frac {1}{3} \\ \frac {1}{2} & 0 & -\frac {1}{2} \\ -\frac {1}{4} & \frac {1}{2} & -\frac {1}{4} \end {bmatrix} \begin {bmatrix} \text {R} \\ \text {G} \\ \text {B} \end {bmatrix} \Leftrightarrow \begin {bmatrix} \text {R} \\ \text {G} \\ \text {B} \end {bmatrix} = \begin {bmatrix} 1 & 1 & -\frac {2}{3} \\ 1 & 0 & \frac {4}{3} \\ 1 & -1 & -\frac {2}{3} \end {bmatrix} \begin {bmatrix} \text {Y} \\ \text {C}_1 \\ \text {C}_2 \end {bmatrix}. \label {eq:optimal} \end {equation}

The YCoCg color transform is orthogonal and the synthesis filters gains4 are 22/9 (for \(\text {Y}\)), 25/9 (for \(\text {C}_1\)) and 22/9 (for \(\text {C}_2\)).

Unfortunately, from a perceptual perspective we must impose (thinking of the subsampling of the chromas) some features in a color transform (such as the influence of the green channel on the luma channel should be high) that violates the orthogonality constrain [?]. For this reason the authors finally propose the transform \begin {equation} \begin {bmatrix} \text {Y} \\ \text {Co} \\ \text {Cg} \end {bmatrix} = \begin {bmatrix} \frac {1}{4} & \frac {1}{2} & \frac {1}{4} \\ \frac {1}{2} & 0 & -\frac {1}{2} \\ -\frac {1}{4} & \frac {1}{2} & -\frac {1}{4} \end {bmatrix} \begin {bmatrix} \text {R} \\ \text {G} \\ \text {B} \end {bmatrix} \Leftrightarrow \begin {bmatrix} \text {R} \\ \text {G} \\ \text {B} \end {bmatrix} = \begin {bmatrix} 1 & 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 & -1 \end {bmatrix} \begin {bmatrix} \text {Y} \\ \text {Co} \\ \text {Cg} \end {bmatrix}, \end {equation} that is near orthogonal5, and has channel gains 3 (\(\text {Y}\)), 2 (\(\text {Co}\)) and 3 (\(\text {Cg}\)).

Again, notice that if the \(\text {RGB}\) values ranges between \(0\) and \(255\) (and rounding to the nearest integer), \(0\le \text {Y}\le 255\), \(-128\le \text {Co}\le 127\) and \(-128\le \text {Cg}\le 127\), and therefore, the number of bits that are necessary to represent each component is \(8\). Therefore, we can use the same QS range for each component. See this notebook.
